untitled , 2021 current work-in-progress
(forum posts, visual code studio)

in the 90's and early 2000's there existed many virtual spaces you could visit as if they were libraries, clubhouses, or meeting places. individuals took pride in the html customization they would undertake to create a certain aesthetic for their virtual space, and the website would be moulded to fit their desired usage. this is in contrast to today's sites like facebook and instagram that provide a concrete aesthetic and function to users that sign up for accounts. with covid impacting outings outside the home, virtual spaces have become even more important in connecting people and providing places to explore.

this project aims to investigate the abstraction of food and their spaces we visit to eat them in the time of quarantine. no longer do we visit a curated physical space, with foodstuffs prepared especially hot or cold, cooked just right. rather, we are left with take-out and delivery, where noodles are clumped, meat is just warm, and crunchy parts have become limp â€” the dishes have become a shadow of what they are meant to actually be. the image of food we cannot make ourselves has become an inaccessible ideal, competing with what we have experienced pre-covid and perfect pictures we view online. here, curated forum posts on topics about changes in taste are displayed to expand and collapse relative to their opening post. upon the click of a button, black body text is toggled off to display selected words that make disjoined poetry about the topic at hand. large, interactive pixelated foods modelled off of notable things personally eaten outside of covid are placed atop all of this, only somewhat looking like the foods they are meant to be. blocking the type from being read, the abstracted food must be moved around by the user such that they ma read what forum branch they have chosen to expand.
this isn't the whole picture, 2020
(premiere pro, photoshop, nikon 5600)

we rely on our trust of the media and people around us to provide information from outside our personal spheres, but this inevitably brings in distortion of facts; plato's allegory poignantly explains this phenomenon. we only know for certain what we experience with our senses, and rely on others outside our personal spheres to provide us information on the experiences they live. the distortion of 3d object to 2d shadow is inevitable, and likewise, so too is the distortion of experiences we do not personally live. similarly, the curated choices a creator makes in communicating a story to us all weave the resulting perception we gain of a situation. without a personal, lived experience, there will always be that distorted perception. seeing for ourselves the lived lives of people outside of our comfort zones, talking to those whose lives differ from ours, and curiously questioning the world around us are methods to combat this. to acknowledge that we rely on the translation of events, means that we acknowledge our own ignorance.

this isn't the whole picture attempts to explore this concept by using a month's worth of daily photo-sequence capture. the new york city (ny, usa) skyline is reconstructed using masks of buildings and skies from a variety of time and day to manipulate the viewer's perception of the time of day and weather taking place. the loud, bustling never-sleeping city of new york is intentionally transformed into a quiet, and still landscape that hides the ever-constant traffic and fast pace of its people. overlapping video and audio of mundane household tasks reinforce this image of new york city. over the course of the video, these clips transform into disturbing visuals like body obsessive scratching. in tandem, a personal transcription of the silent film, metropolis (1927) 1 is displayed at the top of the frame. as the metropolis text gradually reveals a utopia's sinister underbelly, the viewer's perception of this manipulated new york city moves from quiet and dull to disconcerting and anxious, through the coupled change in unsettling audio and visuals.
edc "everyday carry" , 2021
(iphone, regard 3d, blender, folder.app, adobe acrobat)

the every-day-carry comprises of essentials you bring along wherever you go. with the pandemic of '20-'21 requiring at-home isolation, it has seen little to no use. whilst isolating at home for the past year i have realized that i still use a sort of edc whose contents have have transformed with the circumstances: tylenol for head pains, a fragrance burner and its accompanying candle to teleport me outside of my bubble, noise cancelling headphones to silence the noise of a bustling house, and finally, a lipbalm for my constantly-chapped lips. these objects have been translated into 2d forms that, once printed out, can be assembled into paper models, so that one may live a day in quarantine as i do. with their artifact-riddled textures, simplified geometry, and the inevitable mistakes of assembly, the resulting paper models vaguely resemble their original form - as if data has been lost through a game of broken telephone. in the same vein, covid-19 has changed our daily lives to become a ghost of what it once was. the pandemic has become a lens in which our daily lives are refracted and warped. in its distortion of items from 3d to 2d, back to 3d, the composition aims to demonstrate this experience; that even when we attempt to recreate our typical lives within isolated bubbles, they are only poor imitations of what we previously experienced or truly wish to live.
article spread, 2020
(indesign, photoshop)

a pared down article by j. stewart, published online by slate magazine, is organized into a physical magazine spread. nods to the fun, historical article are found in a variety of visual points of interest strewn methodically throughout the spread, meant to draw a reader in as they flip casually through. burnt orange pull-quotes, images of unique buttons, and a sidebar of interesting button facts assist in breaking up the columns of text, providing the reader a break in the sea of ink that is usually a magazine. oppositely positioned illustration (courtesy of i. datskov via shutterstock.com) and article title balance one another out. to further visualize the "sexiness" of the button, kazimir, a striking and playful font by ctsm fonts was chosen for the title and a few other display elements, whilst galvji, a clean sans serif was used for easy reading of body text.
seminar posters, 2020
(graphite, scanner, photoshop, illustrator, indesign)

two posters were created for a mock architecture series.
graphite lines and scans of ink jet type were processed in photoshop and used to provide texture. for a poster advertising a lecture on the industrial revolution's contribution to architecture, i wanted to focus on rebar for its incredible role in advancing architectural ability. having the typographic elements 'swirl' around this central imagery visually reinforces the idea that the industrial revolutions advancement of architecture was directly due to this invention.

because poster 2 was for a post-modernist lecture, i knew i wanted to create a loud and bustling poster. organic, undulating waves with depth were used as a background as post-modernist art and architecture enjoyed liberal use of curves and asymmetry. a scanner was used to further introduce curve and distortion into some type elements, since another trend of post-modernism included the rejection of 'pure' form. though loud, underlays of white/black were used to increase legebility of text atop a noise-riddled texture.
